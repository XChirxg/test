Estimating Standard Deviation of Portfolio

Standard Deviation

Standard deviation is a measure of the dispersion or spread of a set of values. In the context of a portfolio, it measures the volatility or risk of the portfolio.

Formula for Standard Deviation

The formula to calculate standard deviation of a portfolio is:

Standard Deviation = √(∑(Weight of Asset i * (Standard Deviation of Asset i)²))

Example

Suppose you have a portfolio with two assets. Asset 1 has a weight of 0.6 and a standard deviation of 0.1, and Asset 2 has a weight of 0.4 and a standard deviation of 0.2. The standard deviation of the portfolio would be:

Standard Deviation = √(0.6 * (0.1)² + 0.4 * (0.2)²) = √(0.006 + 0.016) = √0.022 ≈ 0.1483

Types of Risk in Portfolio

  • Systematic Risk: Also known as market risk, it is the risk inherent to the entire market or market segment. It cannot be eliminated through diversification.
  • Unsystematic Risk: Also known as specific risk, it is the risk that is specific to a particular company or industry. It can be reduced through diversification.

Need for Estimating Standard Deviation

Estimating the standard deviation of a portfolio is important for investors to understand the risk associated with their investments and to make informed decisions about diversification.

Nature/Features of Standard Deviation

  • Measures the volatility or risk of a portfolio.
  • Helps in assessing the diversification benefits of adding assets to a portfolio.

Advantages of Estimating Standard Deviation

  • Helps investors in managing risk by diversifying their portfolios.
  • Provides a quantitative measure of the risk associated with a portfolio.

Disadvantages of Estimating Standard Deviation

  • Does not account for all types of risk, such as political or economic risk.
  • Relies on historical data, which may not always be indicative of future performance.

Conclusion

Estimating the standard deviation of a portfolio is essential for investors to manage risk and make informed decisions about diversification, but it should be used along with other measures of risk.
